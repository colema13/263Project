You Never Agreed to This.

Consent has become a legal fiction in the age of mass surveillance. Your face, your movements, your patterns — collected, stored, and shared without your knowledge. The question is whether we can reclaim any meaningful control.

The End of Meaningful Consent

In theory, consent is the cornerstone of privacy law. In practice, it has been systematically hollowed out by the surveillance industry. When a neighbor installs a Ring doorbell that captures the public sidewalk, you did not consent to being filmed. When Clearview AI scraped billions of photos from social media to build a facial recognition database sold to police, you did not consent to being in it. When a retailer uses facial recognition to track your shopping behavior, the fine-print terms buried in their app — that you agreed to by entering the store — are not meaningful consent. Below, we examine three specific breakdowns in how consent is handled in modern surveillance.

Case 01

Clearview AI: The Database You Didn't Join

Clearview AI scraped more than 30 billion photographs from Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, Venmo, and millions of other websites to build the world's largest facial recognition database — without asking anyone. The company then sold access to this database to law enforcement agencies across the United States and abroad. Individuals whose faces appear in that database, which is essentially anyone who has ever been photographed online, had no opportunity to consent, no ability to opt out, and in many cases no way to even know they were included. Multiple countries have ruled Clearview's practices illegal, but the database exists and is actively used in the US. This is what the collapse of consent looks like at scale.

Case 02

The Law Cannot Keep Up

The United States has no comprehensive federal law governing facial recognition. A patchwork of state laws — Illinois' Biometric Information Privacy Act being the strongest — provides protection in some places but leaves vast gaps elsewhere. The EU's GDPR offers stronger biometric protections, but enforcement has been slow and exceptions broad. Meanwhile, the technology has proliferated into airports, schools, workplaces, and public events. By the time a new law is passed, the industry has already normalized a new level of data collection. Legal scholars have argued that the traditional notice-and-consent model is structurally incompatible with always-on surveillance environments, and that we need new frameworks built around data minimization and purpose limitation rather than individual opt-in.

Case 03

Private Networks, Public Spaces

Amazon's Ring has partnered with thousands of local police departments through its Neighbors app, creating a de facto private surveillance network covering residential neighborhoods across the country. Ring footage has been shared with law enforcement — in some cases without a warrant or the homeowner's knowledge — through a now-discontinued portal that police could access directly. Smart doorbells, business security cameras, and HOA surveillance systems are increasingly networked and searchable. The person being filmed in the frame of a neighbor's camera has no right to know, no right to remove themselves, and no recourse if that footage is used in an investigation. Consent, in this infrastructure, simply does not exist.
