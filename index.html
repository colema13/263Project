The Government Is Watching.

With the use of facial recognition technologies, those in charge of making those products should make sure that safety and privacy are a main priority. However, companies like Flock continue to improperly use the data gathered from cameras — and the technology remains prone to errors like misidentifying minorities.

Surveillance technology has quietly expanded into everyday spaces — from street corners to apartment lobbies. The implications for civil liberties are profound and largely undebated. As cameras multiply, the question is no longer whether you are being watched, but who holds that data and for how long.

Facial recognition algorithms have repeatedly demonstrated racial bias in independent audits. Error rates for darker-skinned individuals can be dramatically higher than for lighter-skinned ones, raising serious questions about deploying these tools in policing and public safety contexts where a false positive can ruin a life.

Read more »

Beyond bias, there is the question of consent. Most people filmed by license plate readers, facial recognition cameras, or smart doorbells never agreed to participate in a database. Legal frameworks have struggled to keep pace with how quickly these systems have proliferated in both public and private hands.
